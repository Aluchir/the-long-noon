# The Keeper

## 1. Snapshot
**Essence:** the devoted caretaker of a monument no one remembers the meaning of — the world's last mourner, performing a rite whose grief they can't name.
**Role:** early atmosphere and the player's first concrete "something is wrong here"; gateway to the monument lore thread.
**First met:** **The Sunhollow** (R1) at the village monument; reappears with deeper resonance in **The Stillworks** (R3).

## 2. Surface
A gentle, dignified soul who tends the village monument every day — sweeping it, polishing it, laying fresh Bloom-flowers at its base. Unfailingly kind, a little solemn, the sort of person who makes a place feel cared for. They'll tell you proudly that they keep the monument "because it matters," and invite you to pay your respects.

## 3. The dagger
Ask what the monument commemorates and the Keeper will smile, open their mouth, and find **nothing there.** They don't know. No one does. And yet, some days, mid-sweep, the Keeper **weeps** — quietly, helplessly — and cannot tell you why, and is gently embarrassed by it. The monument is a **memorial to the dead of the world that existed before the Long Noon** — the mothers, children, partners, friends, the entire weight of mortal humanity. The Keeper is the last person still performing the rite of mourning, with the grief intact and the *reason* erased. **They are crying for everyone who ever died, and they think it's just a mood.**

## 4. Appearance & visual design
- Quiet, formal-ish dress relative to the villagers — an unconscious sense of occasion.
- Always with a cloth, a broom, fresh-cut Bloom-flowers; hands stained from polishing.
- The monument itself: weathered, its inscription in the **forgotten script** (the same script the player slowly learns) — readable to the player only late game, when its meaning lands like a hammer.

## 5. Personality & voice
- Soft-spoken, kind, carrying a sourceless gravity.
- Deflects their own tears gracefully; never self-pitying.

**Sample lines:**
- *"I keep it because it should be kept. Isn't that reason enough? It feels like reason enough."*
- *"Don't mind me. It comes over me sometimes, here. Passes. ...I don't know what 'it' is. Funny thing to not know about yourself."*
- (late, player reads the inscription) *"...You can read it? What does it — no. No, tell me slowly. I think I've been waiting a very long time to be told."*

## 6. Backstory & lore
The monument is the single most important lore object in the early game: physical, daily, emotionally legible long before it's intellectually understood. The Keeper is the human face of the game's thesis — **grief outlived its memory.** Their daily rite is the world's muscle-memory of mourning.

## 7. Gameplay role & mechanics
- Anchors the **monument lore thread**: the player gradually learns the forgotten script and can eventually read the inscription, a major story beat.
- Gives gentle, non-combat **tribute requests** (bring specific Bloom-flowers) that teach gathering and seed lore drops.
- Reappears tending a **larger, older monument** in the Stillworks — escalating the mystery (who built memorials, and why, in a world that can't die?).

## 8. Arc across the game
- R1: a poignant atmospheric figure.
- R3: the Stillworks monument recontextualizes the village one — these are graves, or memorials to graves.
- Endgame: reading the inscription to the Keeper is an optional but devastating scene; in a memory-restored ending, the Keeper finally *knows* who they've been mourning. Catharsis or unbearable, depending on the ending.

## 9. Relationships
- **The Newcomer:** the first being in an age to *ask why they grieve* — the spark that begins the world's remembering.
- **The One Who Counts:** unspoken kinship; two people performing forgotten rites of the dead, who sense but can't name what they share.
- **The villagers:** fond of the Keeper, mildly puzzled by the devotion, never curious enough to ask.

## 10. Easter eggs & deep-lore hooks
- The **fresh flowers** the Keeper lays match, exactly, a pre-Noon funerary plant named in recovered texts — instinct surviving meaning.
- Counting the Keeper's visits / the worn grooves on the monument is another oblique measure of how long the Noon has lasted.
- A hidden line on the monument, readable only with the full script, names the Long Noon and the Gardener directly — connecting the village's quiet grief to the cosmic cause.
