# The One Who Counts

## 1. Snapshot
**Essence:** a person making endless tally marks on a wall — millions of them — who is, without knowing it, the last accountant of the dead.
**Role:** mid-game deep-lore figure; one of the game's signature quiet-horror reveals.
**First met:** **The Stillworks** (R3), in a vast quiet hall lined with marked walls.

## 2. Surface
You find them in a cavernous old hall, calmly, methodically scoring small marks into the wall with a worn stylus. They're polite, focused, unbothered by your arrival. Ask what they're doing and they'll say, simply, *"Counting."* Ask what they're counting and they'll pause, genuinely consider it, and admit they're not sure anymore — but it would be wrong to stop. The hall stretches further than you can see, every surface dense with marks.

## 3. The dagger
The marks number in the **millions** — and if the player ever tallies a section and extrapolates (an optional, chilling piece of environmental math), the count matches the **population of the world as it was *before* the Long Noon.** Every mark is a person who once lived and died. The One Who Counts is **grieving every single human death that ever happened**, one stroke at a time, and has forgotten that grief is what it is. They believe it's a task. It's the largest act of mourning imaginable, mistaken by its performer for a chore. They will never finish, because they no longer remember the number they're counting to — only that they must not stop.

## 4. Appearance & visual design
- Spare, worn, hands callused and ink/chalk-stained; eyes calm and very, very tired in a way they don't perceive.
- The **wall** is the real character: receding into dark, marks shifting from crisp-recent to ancient-faded, an ocean of small strokes. Lighting (the Long Noon, stretched and dim here) rakes across them.
- A subtle visual: the oldest marks are in the **forgotten script's** counting-glyphs; the recent ones are crude tallies — even *how to count* has eroded over the ages.

## 5. Personality & voice
- Serene, economical, faintly far-away; not unfriendly, just elsewhere.
- Speaks in short, certain sentences about a thing they're no longer certain of.

**Sample lines:**
- *"Counting. ...What? No, I don't mind company. The marks don't mind either."*
- *"What am I counting? I knew, once. Now I only know it would be a sin to stop before it's done."*
- (late, if the player shares the truth) *"...Each one a person. Each one. I've been — oh. Oh, I see. I thought I was working. I've been weeping with my hands."*

## 6. Backstory & lore
Pre-Noon, fragments suggest a role like a recordkeeper, registrar, or census-keeper — someone whose duty was *to account for people.* When death stopped and memory frayed, the duty outlived the meaning, the way the Keeper's rite did. Together, the Keeper and the One Who Counts are the world's two surviving instruments of mourning: one tends the monument, one keeps the tally. Neither knows what they truly do.

## 7. Gameplay role & mechanics
- A **landmark lore encounter**, not a vendor — designed to stop the player cold.
- Optional **environmental puzzle/insight:** estimating the count (via marked-section math + a recovered census fragment) unlocks a major codex revelation and ties the marks to the monument and the pre-Noon population.
- May give a single, weighty request: bring them a recovered counting-glyph text so they can "count properly again" — restoring, briefly, the dignity of the act.

## 8. Arc across the game
- R3: the reveal that the world's dead were *real and numberless.*
- Endgame: if the player tells them the truth, it's a pivotal emotional beat; in the memory-restored ending, the count finally *means* something and can, at last, end.

## 9. Relationships
- **The Keeper:** unspoken twins of grief; if both threads are completed, a quiet scene connects them.
- **The Newcomer:** the only one who does the math, the only one who could ever tell them what they've been doing.

## 10. Easter eggs & deep-lore hooks
- The **count = pre-Noon population** is a discoverable, datamine-able exact correspondence — a reward for the players who measure.
- The shift from script-glyphs to crude tallies along the wall is a readable **timeline of forgetting** — early ages counted with language, later ages with scratches.
- A faded final line at the hall's deepest end, in full script, reads as a dedication: *"so that no one goes uncounted" —* the original purpose, intact, waiting millions of marks away.
