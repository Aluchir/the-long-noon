# Metaphysics Bible — how the world of The Long Noon works

> The canonical rules of the setting. Everything in lore, design, and art must obey these. When a later doc contradicts this one, this one wins (or this one gets updated deliberately).

---

## The three laws of the world

Everything cascades from one event and its three consequences.

1. **Nothing ends.** No death, no decay, no rot, no entropy in the usual sense. Living things persist indefinitely; so do many non-living processes.
2. **Nothing begins.** No birth. No new life arose after the event — until, recently and inexplicably, the Newcomer (the player).
3. **Nothing is kept.** Memory in immortal minds erodes. Without death to hand off knowledge between generations, and with minds never built to hold centuries, the past dissolves. People retain roughly a decade of coherent memory; everything older fogs into instinct and habit.

These are not magic the player casts. They are the *physics* of the setting. The whole game is the slow, dawning comprehension of what these three laws cost.

---

## The Event — the Long Noon

An age ago (uncountable — that's the point), an alien intelligence the survivors remember only as **the Gardener** arrived. It did not invade. It *tended.* Observing a species that suffered and died constantly, it performed what it understood as an act of mercy: **it removed death.**

The moment it did, the sun stopped. Not destroyed, not extinguished — *paused,* fixed at a gentle golden late afternoon. Time, in the way mortals mean it, ended. The survivors call that endless afternoon **the Long Noon**, or simply "the day," because there has only ever been one since.

The frozen golden light is the visible signature of the Event. It emanates, ultimately, from the Gardener's resting place at the heart of the world (**the First Garden**). Every region's lighting subtly "points home" to it.

## Immortality — the mechanics of not-dying

- **No death from any cause.** Injury, starvation, drowning, falling — none kill. Bodies persist. Pain can be felt (dimmed over centuries), but harm is not terminal.
- **No aging.** Bodies are fixed at whatever state they held at the Long Noon.
- **No reproduction.** A direct consequence: with no death and no scarcity, the biological and social machinery of birth simply stopped. There are no children. There have been no children for an age.
- **Consequence for society:** family, lineage, romantic partnership, and "loved ones" lost their structural basis. Bonds built on "I will lose you" or "I will pass something on" had nothing to hold them up. What remains is pleasant, casual, shallow affection — warmth without stakes.

> **Design implication:** there is **no death state for any character, including the player** (see [Sprint 1 decisions](../SPRINT-1-DECISIONS.md) §4). "Combat" is non-lethal reclamation. Failure means setback, never death.

### The one exception: the Newcomer
The player is **mortal** — the single being the Gardener's gift never covered, because the player *began after* the Event. They age, imperceptibly. They can, one day, die. This is the buried spine of the whole story (see [player character](../characters/player-character.md)).

## Memory erosion — the engine of mystery

The cruelest law, and the one that makes the game possible.

- Immortal minds were never built for eternity. Without death to reset and hand off, memory **silts up and then erodes.** Practical retention is ~a decade.
- **History does not accumulate.** No one remembers the world before the Long Noon. No one remembers the Gardener clearly. No one remembers their own past beyond the recent.
- **What survives is instinct and habit, not knowledge.** The Keeper tends a monument without knowing why. The Forager renames plants whose names are lost. The One Who Counts tallies the dead without knowing they're the dead. Muscle memory and ritual outlive meaning.
- **This is why the story is told through rediscovery, not exposition.** The characters *cannot* explain the world — they don't know it. The player reassembles the truth from fragments, ruins, and inscriptions. The player is, functionally, the world's returning memory.

### Two exceptions to erosion
- **The Rememberer** — one person whose memory never eroded, for reasons even they don't fully know. They carry the entire lost past, alone. The inverse of the player (memory without feeling vs feeling without memory). Key to the True Ending.
- **The forgotten script** — a written language from before the Long Noon. Writing is memory externalized, so it survived where minds didn't. It is unreadable to everyone now. The player gradually learns it, and reading it is how buried truth surfaces (the monument, the tally, the Gardener's words all share this script).

## The Bloom — life that cannot stop

Because nothing decays and nothing dies, **nature never stops growing.** Plants, fungi, and the Gardener's own seeded growths pile endlessly into the **Bloom** — lush, beautiful, and choking. It is not evil; it is life with the brakes removed.

- The Bloom is the world's central physical labor and the game's resource layer: you **prune, harvest, still, and subdue** it.
- Older regions have **older, denser, stranger Bloom** — requiring better tools (the gating mechanic).
- Some Bloom-growths and the Gardener's leftover **constructs** are animate and must be subdued (non-lethally) — this is "combat."
- The Bloom is also a clock of sorts: it regrows over "time" even though the sky never changes.

---

## Time without time

There is no day/night cycle. "Time" is expressed through **activity and change**, not the sky:
- The Bloom regrows.
- NPC routines loop.
- Crafting and travel "take a while."
- The player's own near-imperceptible aging is the only true forward arrow in the world.

Each region holds a slightly different *quality* of the Long Noon — warmer and brighter near the surface (the Sunhollow), longer-shadowed, more amber, and subtly desaturated as you descend toward the First Garden. This "lengthening light" is the visual half of **the forgetting**.

---

## The cosmology, kept deliberately small

We do **not** fully explain the Gardener's origin, species, or home. Its alienness is a feature: the player should leave understanding *what it did and why it thought that was kind*, not *what galaxy it came from*. Keep the mystery cosmic-but-intimate. The story is about grief, memory, and meaning — not space opera.

What the player *can* confirm by the end:
- The Gardener arrived an age ago and removed death as mercy.
- That single act caused the Long Noon, the end of birth, the erosion of memory, and the runaway Bloom.
- It has been waiting ever since, tending, to learn whether it did right.
- The player is the one new, mortal variable it did not foresee — and the one who gets to answer its question.

---

## Canon guardrails (do not break without a decision)
- No character dies on-screen or as a fail-state. Death is a *concept being rediscovered*, not a mechanic that punishes.
- The Gardener is never a villain in tone. Its kindness is the horror.
- Nobody remembers the past cleanly. Any "exposition" must come from artifacts, the script, or the Rememberer — never from a character simply knowing.
- The cute surface always lands before the dark truth. Cute first, dagger second.
- The golden-hour light is sacred. The world is never night, never overcast-grim. Dread is delivered through *wrongness in the warmth,* not darkness.
