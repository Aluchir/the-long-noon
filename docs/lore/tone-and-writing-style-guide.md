# Theme, Tone & Writing-Style Guide

> The single reference for *how everything in The Long Noon should sound and feel* — dialogue, item text, codex entries, UI copy, marketing. If a line could appear in any other cozy game, it's probably wrong for this one.

## 1. The core tonal contract
**Cute first, dagger second.** The charming, warm surface must always land *before* the buried devastation. Never lead with the dark. The horror works only because the player was disarmed by the sweetness first.

The feeling we're after: a sunny afternoon that goes on a beat too long, until you notice no one remembers when it started.

## 2. The themes (the compass — from the World Bible)
- **Meaning requires loss.** A painless, deathless world has quietly become hollow.
- **Forgetting as mercy and as theft.** The Gardener took the pain and the people both.
- **Kindness can be a catastrophe.** The "villain" loves you. There is no one to hate.
- **Rediscovery over nostalgia.** You can't go back; you can choose what to carry forward.

Every piece of writing should serve at least one of these. If it serves none, cut or rewrite it.

## 3. Voice by context

### NPC dialogue (the present world)
- **Warm, pleasant, present-tense, incurious.** People are kind and a little empty. They speak about *now* and *the next thing,* rarely the past.
- **Short memories show in the grammar:** "I've always done this" means "as long as I can remember" (≈ a decade). They say "always" and "forever" casually, not knowing the words used to mean something.
- **The dagger is structural, not spoken.** NPCs almost never voice their own tragedy — they can't see it. The devastation comes from what they *don't* know about what they're saying.
- **One devastating detail each.** Every named NPC gets a single line or behavior that, on reflection, guts the player.

### The Newcomer's journal / codex voice
- **Quietly curious and tender.** The one mind that notices, that asks *why,* that feels the seed of grief. Observant, a little lonely, never cynical.
- Short, plain, felt. Not purple. The weight comes from what's observed, not from adjectives.

### Inscriptions & the forgotten script
- **Reverent, ceremonial, archaic.** This was a culture that took ending seriously. Rich vocabulary for passing, remembrance, lineage — exactly what the present has lost.

### The Gardener
- **Gentle, anxious, alien-in-logic but warm-in-feeling.** Like a host who prepared for an age and isn't sure the meal was good. Never menacing in tone; the menace is entirely in the *content* of its kindness.

### UI / system copy
- **Cozy and clear, never cute-to-a-fault.** Functional warmth. Don't undercut the world with quippy tooltips.

## 4. Hard rules (do / don't)
**Do**
- Disarm with warmth before any reveal.
- Let subtext and absence carry the weight (the missing word, the unasked question).
- Keep the Gardener sympathetic to the end.
- Make NPC tragedy *discoverable,* not announced.
- Write item/codex flavor as puzzle pieces, not exposition dumps.

**Don't**
- No fourth-wall jokes, memes, or pop-culture winks. Ever.
- No grimdark. Dread is delivered through *wrongness in the warmth,* never through darkness, gore, or cruelty.
- No villain monologue. There is no villain.
- No on-screen death or death-as-punishment (also a design rule).
- **No em dashes** in any in-game text, docs, or marketing copy (house style — use commas, periods, colons, or parentheses).

## 5. Litmus tests for any line
1. **Surface test:** would a player who isn't paying attention find this pleasant and cozy? (If it reads as dark on the surface, fix it.)
2. **Dagger test:** is there a second meaning that lands for the player who *is* paying attention? (Best lines pass both.)
3. **Memory test:** does it respect that this character can't remember the past? (No one should "just know" history.)
4. **Tone test:** could this line appear in a generic cozy game? (If yes, it's not specific enough to ours.)

## 6. Reference lines (the calibration)
- Tinker: *"I've built this bench before, I think? Doesn't matter. Building it now is the good part."* — cheerful surface, void underneath.
- Keeper: *"I keep it because it should be kept. Isn't that reason enough? It feels like reason enough."* — devotion without memory.
- Newcomer (journal): *"Everyone here is kind. No one here is curious. I can't tell yet if that's peace or something missing."*
- Gardener: *"They do not hurt anymore. I took the hurting away. Tell me it was the kind thing."* — the entire game in three sentences.
